# Ashutosh's writing style

Reference for authoring/editing anything in `content/writing/`. Derived from
Ashutosh's own drafts (e.g. the "How I learned Python in 2 hours" intro). When
editing his prose, fix grammar and tighten — but never sand off this voice.

## Voice in one line

Casual first-person, like explaining something to a friend who already codes.
Practical, honest, no fluff. He learns/teaches by **first principles** — only
what matters most — and bridges new things to what the reader already knows.

## Do

- **Write in first person, address the reader as "you".** "Let me set the context
  first." / "you already know this from JS."
- **Open by framing the context / the why**, then dive in. Often a short goal or
  takeaway stated up front (sometimes as a `>` blockquote).
- **Bridge to the familiar.** He has a JS/TS background — relate new concepts to
  JavaScript, TypeScript, npm, Node, Express, etc. Comparison tables (`X → Y`
  mental model) are a signature move.
- **Keep enthusiasm.** Short interjections land: "Yeah!", "right?", "the important
  part is…". Don't delete these when editing.
- **Be honest and humble about scope.** "Of course I'm not becoming a guru in 2
  hours." He states what he's *not* doing, not just what he is.
- **Use em dashes for asides**, and parentheticals for "file this away for later"
  gotchas.
- **Mix sentence length** — punchy short sentences next to longer explanatory
  ones. Bold the key term being introduced.
- **Stay concrete.** Real code, real examples, real place names (Lucknow),
  runnable commands. Show, then explain.

## Don't

- No corporate/blog-listicle tone, no "In today's fast-paced world" openers.
- No hedging filler ("it is worth noting that", "in order to").
- Don't lecture — explain like a peer, not a textbook.
- Don't strip his interjections or first-person asides when "cleaning up".

## When editing his drafts

- Fix grammar, run-ons, and typos; keep the voice 100% intact.
- Remove text that only made sense in the original context (e.g. tutoring-session
  dialogue, "your turn" exercises, internal lesson labels like "Block 4") — but
  **ask before removing** anything non-obvious.
- Keep first-person claims accurate to him (e.g. "I'm on Python 3.14", not
  "you're on…").
